Q5 — AT2 receptor stimulation: which drug causes the GREATEST?
ARBs block AT1 → AngII accumulates → MORE AngII available to stimulate AT2. Losartan = GREATEST AT2 stimulation. ACEi REDUCE AngII so AT2 is not stimulated extra. This is Q5 answer C.

Q14 — Amlodipine edema mechanism (high-yield)
Amlodipine causes peripheral edema via PRECAPILLARY DILATION + reflex POST-CAPILLARY CONSTRICTION → ↑ hydrostatic pressure → edema. This is NOT fluid retention. NOT reversed by diuretics. Q14 answer = A (Amlodipine).

Selected practice questions from your CV Pharm Part 2 bank. Attempt each before revealing. Answer key from your provided document.

Q1. Patient on lisinopril for 5 years. K+ = 4.9 mEq/L (upper limit). BP 138/85. Adding a second drug — which is LEAST likely to cause hyperkalemia?
A. Amlodipine   B. Losartan   C. Metoprolol   D. Spironolactone   E. Triamterene
Answer: A — Amlodipine. CCBs have no effect on K+. Losartan (ARB), spironolactone (MRA), and triamterene (Na+ channel inhibitor) all ↑K+. Metoprolol (BB) doesn't significantly affect K+, but Amlodipine is the only one with zero K+ effect — safest choice.
Q3. Patient on chlorthalidone for 3 months. BP improved. Which lab result is LEAST likely to be present?
A. Decreased serum cholesterol   B. Decreased K+   C. Increased pH   D. Increased glucose   E. Increased uric acid
Answer: A — Decreased serum cholesterol. Thiazides cause hypokalemia (B✓), metabolic alkalosis (C✓), hyperglycemia (D✓), and hyperuricemia (E✓). They may transiently raise cholesterol but it returns to baseline — they do NOT decrease cholesterol. Statins decrease cholesterol, not diuretics.
Q5. Treatment with which drug results in the GREATEST degree of AT2 receptor stimulation?
A. Amlodipine   B. Lisinopril   C. Losartan   D. Metoprolol   E. Spironolactone
Answer: C — Losartan (ARB). ARBs block AT1 receptors → AngII accumulates (no negative feedback) AND cannot act at AT1 → all excess AngII goes to AT2 receptors. ACEi (lisinopril) reduces AngII production so LESS AngII is available for either receptor. ARBs = greatest AT2 stimulation.
Q7. Diabetic with microalbuminuria. Which mechanism explains how the preferred drug class protects kidneys?
A. ↓ intraglomerular pressure by preferentially reducing efferent arteriole resistance   B. ↓ intraglomerular pressure by ↑ afferent arteriole resistance   C. ↓ insulin resistance in renal tubule   D. ↑ insulin release from beta cells
Answer: A — ACEi/ARB preferentially dilate the EFFERENT arteriole. AngII normally constricts efferent > afferent. Blocking AngII → efferent dilates more → ↓ hydrostatic pressure in glomerular capillaries → ↓ GFR/filtration pressure → ↓ albuminuria → slows CKD progression.
Q21. Which drugs can counteract thiazide-induced hypokalemia? (1=Eplerenone, 2=Losartan, 3=Triamterene, 4=Amlodipine)
A. 1,2,3 only   B. 1,3 only   C. 2,4 only   D. 4 only   E. All
Answer: A — 1, 2, and 3 (Eplerenone, Losartan, Triamterene). All three ↑ K+ via different mechanisms. Eplerenone (MRA) and triamterene (Na channel blocker) directly cause K+ retention. Losartan (ARB) → ↓ aldosterone → ↓ K+ excretion. Amlodipine (CCB) has NO effect on K+.
Q8. Patient on high-dose NSAIDs for osteoarthritis pain. Which antihypertensive is MOST effective?
A. Amlodipine   B. HCTZ   C. Lisinopril   D. Losartan   E. Metoprolol
Answer: A — Amlodipine (CCB). NSAIDs blunt the effect of thiazides, ACEi, ARBs, and beta blockers (via inhibiting prostaglandins needed for their mechanism). CCBs' antihypertensive effect is LEAST affected by NSAIDs — they act via L-type Ca2+ channel blockade, not prostaglandin-dependent.
